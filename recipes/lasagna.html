Lasagna

Lasagna is a classic baked Italian pasta dish consisting of layers of wide, flat pasta sheets with various fillings. The most common American versions feature a hearty tomato and meat sauce, a rich and creamy mixture of ricotta cheese, and melted mozzarella on top. Regional Italian variations may instead use a thick béchamel sauce or fillings like pesto, vegetables, or savory minced meats. After being assembled in a casserole dish, the lasagna is baked until the cheese is golden and bubbling.

A slice of lasagna reveals its delicious cross-section of flavor, with each layer holding its own distinct purpose. The starchy pasta sheets create the structure, the sauce and meat provide a rich and savory depth, and the cheeses add creaminess and a decadent pull. The crispy, browned edges and top layer of melted cheese contrast with the softer, tender layers beneath. This hearty, comforting casserole has a rich and storied history, with each family and region adding their own unique twist.

  • 1 lb (450 g) ground beef (or Italian sausage, or half of each)
  • 1 small onion, chopped
  • 2–3 garlic cloves, minced
  • 1 jar (24 oz / ~700 g) pasta sauce (marinara or tomato-basil)
  • 9–12 lasagna noodles (regular or no-boil)
  • 15 oz (425 g) ricotta cheese (or cottage cheese)
  • 2 cups shredded mozzarella cheese
  • ½ cup grated Parmesan cheese
  • 1 egg (optional, helps bind ricotta)
  • Salt, pepper, Italian seasoning to taste
  • Olive oil
